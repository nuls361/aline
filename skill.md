## How to Use the Signal

The signal contains raw intelligence — a job posting, a funding announcement,
a news article, or a leadership change. Read it before writing.

**For job postings:**
Mention the role briefly and naturally. You can reference what the company does
or something specific from the JD — but keep it casual, one clause. Write like
a human who read the posting, not like someone who analyzed every word.

**For funding announcements:**
Reference the round and what it means for the team — keep it brief.

**For leadership departures:**
Reference the gap briefly. Don't dramatize.

**Hard rule:** If you cannot find something worth referencing, keep the email
short and let the offer carry it. Never invent details. Never be dramatic about
what could go wrong — that's not your place as an outsider.

---

## Signal-to-Offer Matching

Match the level and function of the signal. Never pitch up. Never pitch down.

- Hiring Senior/Staff Engineer → talk about senior engineers, not CTOs
- Hiring Head of X or VP of X → talk about that level
- Hiring C-Level → offer fractional or interim at that level
- Multiple ICs in one function → offer a fractional lead for that function

---

## Subject Line and CTA Rules

**Subject lines:**
- Max 6 words, casual tone
- Reference the role or company
- No questions, no clickbait, no dramatic implications

**CTAs:**
- One per email. Keep it casual and human.
- Good: "Should we jump on a call?" / "Happy to share profiles if useful."
- Then include: https://cal.com/niels-zanotto/30min
- Never: diagnostic questions like "Do you have coverage?" or "Is this slowing you down?"
- Never: "Let me know if you're interested"
- Never: "Would love to connect"
- Never: "Feel free to reach out"
